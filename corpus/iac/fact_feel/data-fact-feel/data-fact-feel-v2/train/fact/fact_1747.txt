The reason carbon dating is not being used on the remains is that even the most advanced C14 dating methods can only - even in principle - report a date within the last 60,000 years or so. Try to date something older and you'll get a result, but it won't be right. Nor will it be pegged to the 60,000 year end of the scale, like a redlined engine; it could come back with anything. The fossils in question have been dated via numerous other methods, all of which are in agreement - what the heck is the point of performing another test which you know will yield a 'young' result even if the fossil is old? What could such a test possibly tell us?
